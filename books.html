More books

What I enjoy the most about my readings is learning something new and many times I opt for somewhat unusual books like...

The Immortalization Commission

John Gray (Sexto Piso, 2014)

A story where science was used against itself and became a channel for magic. In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, an antideath movement emerged in Russia with members such as the Nobel writer Maksim Gorki and the trade minister Leonid Krasin. Their belief that technology could raise the dead led them to create the commission for immortalization to preserve the remains of Vladimir Lenin -today, a 97-year-old mummy-.

The Heroes of the Greeks

Karl Kerenyi (Atalanta, 2009)

Kerenyi wrote this book convinced that the time had come to make a mythology whose fundamental interest was the study of the human being from any branch of knowledge. Thus, it goes against the great manuals for specialists in classical studies or the books of classical myths for children, remodeled and falsified. Mythology is also a testament to modern humanistic thought.

The books of Earthsea

Ursula K. Le Guin (Minotauro, 2020)

I know this reading is not unusual at all, but I want to include it because it is the kind I do only for entertainment, pleasure and imagination. I love Ursula.
